Behind Each Dish

The Story & the Technique

Menu One — Appetizer

Classic Caprese Salad

Fresh mozzarella and vine-ripe tomatoes, topped with fresh homemade basil pesto.

Preparation Notes

A dish this simple lives or dies on the quality of three ingredients — so each one is chosen and handled with real care: mozzarella sliced to order rather than pre-cut, tomatoes selected at peak ripeness, and the pesto made fresh by hand rather than store-bought, finished with good olive oil just before serving.

Menu One — Entrée

Lamb Chops with Ratatouille-Stuffed Roasted Butternut Squash

Served on a bed of curry coconut sauce with mint jelly.

Preparation Notes

The lamb is seared to develop a proper crust before finishing to precise temperature. The ratatouille is built the classical French way — each vegetable cooked separately to preserve its own texture before coming together — then used to stuff the roasted squash. The curry coconut sauce is reduced slowly to concentrate flavor, and the mint jelly is made in-house.

Menu One — Entrée

Pan-Seared Chicken Breast

Under a golden herb crust, over roasted garlic mashed potatoes and sautéed vegetables, finished with a Dijon mustard cream sauce.

Preparation Notes

The breast is coated in a herb and breadcrumb crust and pan-seared so the crust sets golden and crisp, then finished gently so the chicken underneath stays moist — the crust is there to protect the meat as much as to season it. The garlic is roasted whole before it goes into the potatoes, mellowing its sharpness into something sweeter. The Dijon cream sauce is built as a true pan sauce — deglazed right in the same pan, capturing everything left behind from the sear.

Menu One — Entrée

Fried Whole Red Snapper with Rice, Sautéed Vegetables, and Jalapeños Aioli

Scored and fried whole so the skin crisps while the flesh stays moist, with the aioli served on the side.

Preparation Notes

Cooking a fish whole is the harder path, and the reason is heat: the bone slows it down, so the skin has to crisp without the flesh drying out. The snapper is scored to the bone so it cooks evenly through, and the oil is held at a steady temperature rather than let to drift — too cool and it turns greasy, too hot and the outside colors before the inside is done. The aioli is emulsified by hand, with the jalapeño folded in at the end so the heat stays bright instead of cooking away.

Menu Two — Appetizer

Baby Arugula and Belgian Endive Salad

Topped with goat cheese, walnuts, and cherry tomato, with balsamic vinaigrette on the side.

Preparation Notes

Endive is sliced fresh to order — cut too early and it turns bitter and brown. The walnuts are toasted just before serving for a warm crunch against the cold greens, and the vinaigrette is emulsified fresh and served on the side, so the salad stays crisp until the moment it's eaten rather than wilting under the dressing.

Menu Two — Entrée

Grilled Swordfish

Served over seasonal vegetables and potato purée.

Preparation Notes

Swordfish is lean and unforgiving — a minute too long and it turns dry and chalky. It goes on a hot grill and comes off fast, marked on the outside while the center is still just short of done, then rests so it finishes on carryover heat rather than over the flame. The vegetables are cut to sizes that cook at the same rate, so nothing is mush by the time the slowest piece is ready, and the potato purée is passed smooth and finished with butter off the heat.

Menu Two — Entrée

Pan-Seared Grouper with Shrimp and Niçoise Salsa

On a bed of mashed potatoes with lemon butter sauce.

Preparation Notes

The grouper is laid into a hot pan and then left alone — the fillet releases on its own once the crust has formed, and moving it before that is what tears it. The shrimp are cooked separately so they stay just firm rather than turning rubbery while they wait. The Niçoise salsa of olives, capers and tomato is built cold and spooned over at the end, so its acidity cuts the lemon butter underneath rather than cooking into it.

Menu Two — Entrée

Stuffed Flounder with Garlic Shrimp

Baked in white wine, served over a vegetable ragoût with dill cream sauce.

Preparation Notes

The flounder is butterflied and stuffed by hand, with the shrimp sautéed in garlic beforehand so it's already full of flavor before it goes in. Baking gently in white wine keeps the delicate fish moist, and the dill cream sauce is finished light, so it doesn't overpower the fish itself.
